## Short Summaries of Key Works

### 1 · Crespi & González-Gallardo (2025): Text First! Multimodal Data Augmentation...

**Full Title:** «Text First! Multimodal Data Augmentation for Automated Recognition of Handwritten Premodern Italian Manuscripts»
**In:** *Critical Approaches to Automated Text Recognition* (eds. Terras, Gooding, Ames, Nockels), Facet Publishing.
**Authorship:** Serena Carlamaria Crespi & Carlos-Emiliano González-Gallardo (Crespi authored all sections except 1.4 and 1.6).

The chapter critically examines the application of Artificial Intelligence, specifically Handwritten Text Recognition (HTR) and Computer Vision, to historical document analysis. It highlights the transition of these tools from experimental prototypes to foundational research infrastructure. While acknowledging the success of platforms like Transkribus, eScriptorium, and Kraken, Crespi points out the structural limitations of current HTR models when faced with the irregular, hybrid, and highly personalized scripts typical of early modern (16th-18th century) Italian manuscripts. 

The paper details the specific palaeographic challenges of this era: the loosening of formal script systems, increased individualization of handwriting, spatial instability (ligatures, overlapping lines), and significant intra-scribe variation. To overcome the bottleneck of resource-intensive manual ground-truth creation for these complex scripts, the authors propose multimodal data augmentation strategies to improve the training and generalization capabilities of automated recognition models.

**Methodological Character:** Critical evaluation of AI infrastructure; applied computational palaeography; development of technical solutions for complex historical datasets.

### 2 · Crespi (2026): Manuscript beyond print: exploring 17th-century manuscript culture...

**Full Title:** «Manuscript beyond print: exploring 17th-century manuscript culture in Florence through a multilevel analysis»
**In:** A. Campagnolo, E. Pierazzo (eds.), *Approaches to Digital Codicology: interdisciplinarity and intersections*, Brepols.

This article challenges the prevailing historiographical assumption that manuscript production became obsolete, clandestine, or purely private following the invention of the printing press. Employing an approach framed as "Digital Codicology," Crespi presents a quantitative study of 17th-century manuscript culture in Florence. 

The research is based on a multi-level data model designed to analyze over 5,000 manuscripts across five major Florentine libraries. By integrating codicological, textual, and socio-historical data, the analysis reveals a thriving, highly organized manuscript ecosystem. Key findings include the robust production of high-literary and historical works in prestige formats (folio and quarto) dedicated to patrons. Most notably, the study identifies 'serial' manuscript production—such as standardized copies of Lorenzo Lippi's *Malmantile Racquistato* attributed to Medici court calligrapher Valerio Spada—proving the existence of a semi-industrial manuscript market that successfully coexisted with print.

**Methodological Character:** Digital codicology; quantitative and statistical analysis of large datasets; distant reading combined with material book history.

## Monograph (Accepted): *Manuscript Culture in Early Modern Florence*

**Publisher:** Brepols Publishers (Digital Manuscript and Book Studies series)
**Status:** Formally accepted (Letter from A. Campagnolo, Feb 2026); manuscript delivery expected Dec 2026.
**Length:** Approx. 80,000 words

### Content and Argument

The monograph represents the culmination of Crespi's quantitative codicological research. It systematically dismantles the notion that the 17th-century manuscript was merely a residual medium. Treating Florence as a "privileged observatory" due to its status as a cultural hub and center for academies, the book reconstructs the contours of an entire manuscript ecosystem: what texts were copied, in what physical formats, for which audiences, and through which circulation networks.

### Digital Methodology & In-Depth Analysis

The core of the monograph relies on the aggregation and datafication of a massive corpus (>5,000 codices from >40 collections). 

**Data Modeling and Quantitative Codicology:** 
Crespi builds upon the quantitative traditions of Bozzolo and Ornato, expanding them through modern relational data modeling and computational "distant reading." By creating a structured dataset encompassing materiality (format, paper, hands), textual profiles (genre, authorship), and social profiles (patronage, ownership), she moves beyond isolated case studies.

**Network and Pattern Analysis:**
The methodology allows for the mapping of bibliographic data to reveal systematic regularities:
- Recurring correlations between specific codicological formats and literary genres.
- The sustained, wide circulation of texts that were deliberately kept in manuscript form and never printed.
- The identification of professional infrastructures supporting specialized manuscript production (e.g., the workshop of Valerio Spada), indicating a parallel economy to typography.

The book explicitly addresses the epistemological challenges of this approach, distinguishing statistical inference from documented description, and demonstrating how macro-scale quantitative results can be effectively integrated with micro-historical, autoptic close reading of the material objects.

## Overall Assessment

Crespi's scholarly profile is highly coherent, situated at the cutting edge of **computational philology and digital codicology**. Her work operates simultaneously across three crucial levels:

1.  **Material & Historical Scholarship:** Deep expertise in early modern Italian literature, paleography, and traditional codicology.
2.  **Methodological Innovation:** Pioneering quantitative, data-driven approaches ("distant reading") to massive manuscript corpora, shifting the field from localized case studies to systemic, ecosystem-level analysis.
3.  **Digital Infrastructure & AI:** Active development and publication of technical resources, including HTR models, data augmentation code, technical manuals, and digital critical editions (e.g., the *Gesuiteide* TEI edition and the MIV17 database).

Her interdisciplinary approach successfully bridges the gap between traditional humanist inquiry and advanced computational techniques, making her a strong representative of modern digital humanities practice. The forthcoming monograph with Brepols serves as a foundational theoretical and applied work in this domain.
